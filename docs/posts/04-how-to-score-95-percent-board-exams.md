## Content

Ninety-five per cent in board exams. A lot of people assume that means twenty hours a day. It doesn't. It's clearer planning, full syllabus coverage, smart revision, and writing answers the way examiners want to see them. This guide is for anyone aiming high in CBSE or state boards—practical stuff that works.

Board exams aren't a mystery. Fixed syllabus; questions usually from the textbook and sample papers. Cover everything thoroughly, revise properly, present answers well—a high score is realistic. Plenty hit 95% or above without burning out. They had a plan and dodged the usual mistakes.

**First step:** official syllabus and marking scheme from your board's website. See which chapters carry more marks and which topics show up every year. Give those more time. Don't skip anything—one question from a chapter you left out can cost you. Use the syllabus as a checklist; tick off each topic as you finish.

**Books.** For CBSE, NCERT is the base. Every chapter, diagrams, examples, in-text and end-of-chapter questions. For state boards, the recommended textbook is your main source. Board papers often sit very close to the prescribed book. Master that first. Extra references can wait.

**Timetable.** One that's actually doable. Split the syllabus across the months or weeks you have. Tough subjects get more time, easier ones less—but every subject gets some. Block time for revision. Simple pattern: first round complete syllabus; second round revise and solve papers; third round quick revision of formulae and important points. Stick to it; only change when something's clearly not working.

**Notes.** They save you in revision. Don't copy—your own words. Bullet points, headings, maybe a diagram. Definitions, formulae, important dates, key concepts. Subject-wise and chapter-wise so you can find things fast in the last weeks.

**Previous years' papers.** Gold. They show question pattern, how marking works, which topics keep coming. Solve at least the last five years per subject. Time yourself—get used to writing for three hours straight. After each paper, see where you lost marks and fix those gaps. Sample papers from your board help too; they often reflect the latest pattern.

**Presentation.** It matters. Examiners see hundreds of sheets. Clear handwriting, headings where they help, underlined key terms—it adds up. Long answers: short intro, main points, conclusion. Numerical: show steps. Space between answers. Neat and structured can nudge you into the next grade.

**In the hall.** Read the paper first. Do what you're sure about before sinking into the hard ones. Don't pour too much time into one question. Stuck? Leave space, come back. Keep 10–15 minutes at the end to check. Divide the three hours roughly by marks—more marks, more time.

**Revision.** Without it you'll forget. At least two full revisions before the exam. First: all chapters. Second: weak areas, important formulae, definitions. Use short notes. Diagrams and labelled figures—they're asked often.

Eat properly. Sleep 7–8 hours. Short breaks while studying. Sacrificing sleep for last-minute cramming usually backfires. Before the exam, stay calm. Trust your preparation. And avoid long discussions with friends about questions right before you go in—it can shake your confidence.

What pulls scores down? Skipping topics. No revision. Ignoring previous papers. Bad time management in the hall. Messy answer writing. Guess papers or shortcuts. Cover the full syllabus. Clear plan, good notes, enough practice, calm execution—95% is within reach for many.

**Day before.** Don't learn new topics. Skim notes and formulae. Pack bag: admit card, ID, pens. Bed on time. On the day: light meal, reach a bit early, read the paper once before you start. You've prepared—trust that. One paper didn't go well? Don't let it bleed into the next. Each paper is a fresh start.
